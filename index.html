What is a crypto card?

A crypto card lets you spend digital assets using regular card rails (Visa, Mastercard, etc.). Behind the scenes, your crypto is converted to fiat before or at the moment of purchase.

  • • You pay with a physical card, virtual card or wallet
  • • The merchant still receives local currency
  • • Conversion happens via the card provider or exchange partner
  • • Some products add rewards paid back in crypto

Main crypto-to-card models

Custodial crypto card

Provider holds your crypto. When you spend, they auto-sell just enough to cover the transaction.

Stablecoin spending

You keep value in stablecoins (e.g. USD-pegged), then convert to fiat at swipe/tap time.

Preloaded balance

You top up a card balance from crypto in advance, then spend from that fixed fiat balance.

On-chain loyalty

Traditional card, but rewards are paid out as tokens or crypto instead of points.

Fees, spreads and limits

Cost element What to check
FX spread Hidden mark-up between crypto price and executed rate.
Conversion fee Percentage or flat fee on each crypto-to-fiat conversion.
Network fees On-chain transaction costs when you move assets in/out.
ATM & withdrawal Extra fees for cash withdrawals or high-risk merchants.
Daily/monthly limits Maximum volume you can spend or convert per period.

Risk, region and regulation

Crypto cards are not available everywhere, and rules can change quickly. Products may differ significantly by region.

  • • KYC and AML checks are almost always required
  • • Some countries restrict or ban certain crypto products
  • • Provider risk: what happens if the exchange or issuer fails?
  • • Tax treatment of each conversion depends on your jurisdiction
